\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate \svdomp\ against \vpd, \bsf-W (cold and SVD-warm-started), and an SVD-scaffolded trainable variant on the same 4-layer \textsc{LlamaSimpleMLP} used in the \vpd\ paper, extracted from Goodfire's public checkpoint. All 24 target modules (attention $q, k, v, o$ projections and MLP up- and down-projections across 4 layers) are decomposed at the module-specific dictionary sizes $C$ and sparsity $k$ specified in \citet{bushnaq2026vpd}. Real-activation experiments were run on the actual Goodfire-67M model of \citet{bushnaq2026vpd} via a Modal-hosted T4 GPU pipeline (\texttt{modal\_goodfire.py} in the supplement, wandb-authenticated), and cross-scale on Pythia-70M and Pythia-160M \citep{biderman2023pythia} for public reproducibility without authentication. Full code, tests (42 synthetic-data property and integration tests), and reproduction commands are provided in the anonymized supplementary.

\subsection{Sparse-Reconstruction Dominance vs.\ VPD}
\label{sec:experiments-vpd}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/svd_omp_vs_vpd_scatter.png}
\caption{\svdomp\ vs.\ \vpd\ on the 24 target modules. Each point is one weight matrix; below the $y = x$ line means \svdomp\ wins. \svdomp\ wins every per-metric comparison 24 / 24 on sparse reconstruction, faithfulness, and coherence; and 18 / 24 on stability (all six losses are attention $v_\text{proj}$ / $o_\text{proj}$; see Sec.\ \ref{sec:experiments-vpd}).}
\label{fig:vpd-scatter}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:vpd-scatter} shows the four-panel comparison. \svdomp\ is Pareto-dominant on 18 of 24 matrices and wins the per-metric comparison 24 / 24 on sparse reconstruction, faithfulness, and coherence. The six losses on support stability are attention $v_\text{proj}$ (4) and $o_\text{proj}$ (2) modules, exactly the modules whose compressed singular spectra Davis-Kahan predicts as failure modes (Sec.\ \ref{sec:theory}). On per-input support diversity, \svdomp\ achieves 256 / 256 distinct top-$k$ supports on every module tested; \vpd's trained static gate produces the same support for every input.

\subsection{Six-Way Comparison}
\label{sec:six-way}

To isolate the causal factor behind analytic vs.\ trained wins, we run the following six methods on the 24 modules under matched dictionary size and sparsity: \svdomp\ (analytic 1D), block-\svdomp\ (analytic block), scaffold-trainable \svdomp\ (frozen $V, U$; $2M$ learned scalars), \vpd\ (trained 1D), \bsf-W cold (block trained, random init), and \bsf-W warm (block trained, SVD-initialized). Table~\ref{tab:six-way} reports pairwise wins on sparse reconstruction.

Two conclusions follow. First, analytic \svdomp\ and block-\svdomp\ dominate every trained baseline. Second, \bsf\ warm-started from the SVD dominates random-init \bsf\ on 24 / 24 modules under identical training budget. Since these two methods share every hyperparameter except initialization, initialization from the SVD is the causal factor. Training on top of a random dictionary is a costly way to (partially) recover a decomposition that a single SVD supplies exactly.

\subsection{Non-Frobenius Escape: When Training Helps}
\label{sec:non-frobenius-exp}

Eckart-Young caps rank-$k$ reconstruction of $W$ but not composed nonlinear objectives. We construct adversarial (weight, downstream) pairs $(W, W_\text{next})$: $W$ has two clearly separated singular-value tiers --- loud ($\sigma = 10$, four atoms) and quiet ($\sigma = 2$, four atoms) --- and $W_\text{next}$ projects onto the quiet band. Analytic \svdomp\ picks the loud block on 96--98\% of inputs (largest projection norm), but the loud block's contribution is zero after $W_\text{next}$. The trainable correction learns to select the quiet block instead.

\centering

Setup & Mean MSE reduction & Substantive wins \\
\midrule
Adversarial synth. $W_\text{next}$ &  \textbf{73.9\%} & 24 / 24 \\
Real neighboring layers            &  \textbf{16.3\%} & 24 / 24 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Downstream MSE reduction from the non-Frobenius trainable correction vs.\ analytic \svdomp\ on the 24 real Goodfire-67M weight matrices. Adversarial: hand-constructed $W_\text{next}$ projects onto low-singular-value directions of $W$ where the analytic top-$\sigma$ selection is provably suboptimal. Real: neighboring-layer weight matrices in Goodfire's model, less structured misalignment. Both regimes win 24 / 24 but effect size scales with how much the downstream operator hides signal outside the top singular directions.}
\label{tab:non-frobenius}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:non-frobenius} reports both an adversarial upper bound (73.9\% mean MSE reduction on hand-constructed $W_\text{next}$ that maximally misaligns with $W$'s SVD) and a real-world lower bound (16.3\% mean reduction on Goodfire's actual neighboring-layer $W_\text{next}$). Both regimes yield 24 / 24 substantive wins; the effect size depends on how much the downstream operator preferentially routes signal through low-singular-value directions. The selection flip on the adversarial construction is diagnostic: analytic picks the "loud" block on $>$96\% of inputs, trained picks it on $<$10\%. This is the concrete regime where trained decomposition beats analytic --- when the objective composes $W$ with structure the SVD does not capture.

\subsection{BSF's Concept-Dimensionality Plateau, Without Training}
\label{sec:stable-rank}

\bsf\ reports the stable rank of learned blocks plateauing at $\sim 4$ regardless of block size $K \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16\}$ on DINOv3 vision activations \citep{goodfire2026bsf}, and interprets this as evidence that vision concepts are 2--4-dimensional. We reproduce this sweep on real activations from three language models: Goodfire-67M (the interpretability-benchmark model of \citet{bushnaq2026vpd}), Pythia-70M, and Pythia-160M \citep{biderman2023pythia}, from 352--2048 tokens over 32 diverse prompts spanning science, history, mathematics, code, and the arts.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.98\linewidth]{figures/stable_rank_real_lm.png}
\caption{Stable rank vs.\ block size $K$ on real Pythia-160M mid-layer MLP activations. Analytic \svdomp\ blocks (no training) track SVD-warm-started \bsf-W to identical values across all $K$; cold-init \bsf-W is lower at moderate $K$ because it is under-trained. Both plateau below the dashed full-rank-$= K$ line, at $\sim 3.87$ --- essentially matching \bsf's vision-model plateau of $\sim 4$.}
\label{fig:stable-rank}
\end{figure}

\caption{Mean per-block stable rank of analytic \svdomp\ across three real language models at increasing scale, alongside \bsf's reported vision numbers (Fig.~3 of \citet{goodfire2026bsf}). Every LM row uses real activations extracted from a mid-layer MLP. At $K = 16$ the plateau climbs monotonically with model size: 1.74 on Goodfire-67M, 2.10 on Pythia-70M, 3.87 on Pythia-160M --- essentially matching \bsf's vision value at the 160M scale. SVD-warm-started \bsf-W tracks analytic \svdomp\ to identical values at every $K$ on every model (verified in \texttt{results/real\_stable\_rank.json} and \texttt{stable\_rank\_real\_lm.json}; omitted from the table for brevity). \label{tab:plateau}

Figure~\ref{fig:stable-rank} and Table~\ref{tab:plateau} report the sweep at three language-model scales alongside \bsf's reported vision numbers. Two observations. First, analytic \svdomp\ blocks (no training) match SVD-warm-started \bsf-W to identical values at every $K$ on every model (verified in the released JSON results; omitted from the table for brevity); cold-init \bsf-W is lower at moderate $K$ because it is under-trained. This is evidence that the plateau is a property of the activation distribution and not of the featurizer. Second, the plateau climbs monotonically with model size: from Goodfire's small interpretability-benchmark model (67M, 4 layers) at 1.74, through Pythia-70M at 2.10 and Pythia-160M at 3.87, essentially reaching \bsf's reported vision plateau ($\sim$4.0) at the 160M scale. This is consistent with larger models representing higher-dimensional concept structure and offers a cross-scale, cross-modality replication of \bsf's headline finding without training.
